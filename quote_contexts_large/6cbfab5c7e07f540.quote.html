3.1 OUTLINE - The text editor operates in a multi-modal manner, constantly both personal and general. - The personal allows for a constantly shifting perspective throughout the writing. This perspective is established through the placement of it's theoretical stance by the choice of a handful of core references which take the curating role for the textual indices that are shown. This textual environment in turn can also take a grounding role for the projection onto the imagery.

The personal perspective also allows for a sense of intimacy and immediacy, as if the reader is being addressed directly by the writer. This can be particularly effective in creating a sense of urgency or emotional resonance, as the reader is drawn into the writer's inner world. The general perspective, on the other hand, provides a broader context and helps to ground the writing in a larger reality, preventing it from becoming too insular or solipsistic. By oscillating between these two perspectives, the text editor can create a rich and nuanced texture that engages the reader on multiple levels.

Like other media, prose can view scenes from anywhere in the physical world, but it also adds subjective angles within a character’s mental world. Once the prose writer makes a choice of person (first, third, or the eccentric second), her eye moves from that angle like a spotlight. The author holds our perception in her fist. As we follow her sentences, she takes us where she wishes: through the places, times, and societies of her world; or into a single character’s thought-filled depths, there to witness rationalizations, self-deceptions, and dreams; or deeper yet, into the character’s subconscious to reveal her raw appetites, nightmarish terrors, and lost memories.[Mckee, Robert *Dialogue: The Art of Verbal Action for Page, Stage, and Screen*.
