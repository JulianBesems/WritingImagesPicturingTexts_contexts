In concrete terms, equality appears as duplication and mirroring across modalities; measurement as commensuration within textual and visual traces; proportionality as tuned correspondences that hold unlike things together in workable ratios. The situated cast shows this in practice. By setting an authored selection against a large, generic field, the cast turns vague categories into live concepts. An umbrella, seen through Studio Ghibli, becomes a Ghibli umbrella: an object with narrative, weight, and gesture. It means that a concept can stop being an abstract noun and start behaving like something with a particular weather, pacing, and atmosphere. The umbrella example is telling precisely because it is banal: once the umbrella passes through a cast of Ghibli films it ceases to be a generic token and becomes a specific one, a Ghibli umbrella,

The same process unfolds with the concept of 'city'. Initially, it means a sprawling metropolis with skyscrapers, streets, and traffic; but through the cast of Hayao Miyazaki's films, it transforms into a Miyazaki city: a place with winding canals, ancient forests, and a sense of community. The city ceases to be a mere urban infrastructure and becomes a specific, cinematic entity. This recontextualization of the familiar not only highlights the agency of the author but also underscores the power of the situated cast in shaping our understanding of the world.
