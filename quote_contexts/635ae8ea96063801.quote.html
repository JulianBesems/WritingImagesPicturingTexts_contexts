2.4 MATHEMATICAL INTERPRETATION; SET VS GROUP Cross-modal machine learning already moves fluently between objects and indices: images and texts are encoded as points, distances, neighbours, clusters. Yet the outcome is most often acted upon as an organised set, a populated space in which elements are placed in proximity to one another, without an equally explicit account of the operations by which this space is to be traversed, transformed, or made productive. If the multimodal gap is not an anomaly to be eliminated but a condition to be inhabited, then the question shifts from what elements are there? to what actions are available?, from membership to operation. Through the difference between two basic mathematical frameworks, sets and groups, we can establish a mathematical way of looking at this multi modal separation. A set defines elements by membership; what belongs, and what does not. In contrast, an algebraic structure like a group and by extension, a ring, or field defines not only the elements but also the operation by which they interact. It is these operations that structure its generative capacity. This distinction becomes critical when considering how modalities relate. In a set-theoretical view, modalities might be mapped or translated from one to another, assuming that correspondence can be made stable. But in an operational view, modalities are defined by what they do: their ability to transform, combine, and produce effects. In an architectural sense; the section does not translate the plan; it transforms the problem by altering the operational frame.

In the context of multimodal machine learning, sets and groups can be seen as two different ways of bridging the gap between modalities. Sets can be used to map or translate modalities, assuming a stable correspondence can be established. On the other hand, groups offer a more operational perspective, where modalities are defined by the operations they perform and the effects they produce. This distinction has significant implications for how multimodal machine learning systems are designed and used. For example, a set-theoretical approach might focus on developing algorithms that can translate between modalities, whereas an operational approach might emphasize the development of algorithms that can perform specific operations across modalities.  This distinction also has implications for how we understand the relationship between modalities. In a set-theoretical view, modalities might be seen as separate entities that can be mapped or translated from one to another. In contrast, an operational view sees modalities as interconnected systems that are defined by their operations and interactions.  This perspective can lead to a more nuanced understanding of the multimodal gap and how it can be bridged.  By recognizing the distinction between sets and groups, multimodal machine learning can move beyond a simple translation or mapping approach and towards a more sophisticated and operational understanding of how modalities relate.
