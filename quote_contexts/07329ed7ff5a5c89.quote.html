This synthesis of the manifold of sensible intuition, which is possible and necessary a priori, can be called figurative (synthesis speciosa), as distinct from that which would be thought in the mere category in regard to the manifold of an intuition in general, and which is called combination of the understanding (synthesis intellectualis); both are transcendental, not merely because they themselves proceed a priori but also because they ground the possibility of other cognition a priori. Yet the figurative synthesis, if it pertains merely to the original synthetic unity of apperception, i. e. , this transcendental unity, which is thought in the categories, must be called, as distinct from the merely intellectual combination, the transcendental synthesis of the imagination.[Immanuel Kant, Paul Guyer (Editor, Allen W. Wood (Editor Translator), and Translator) *Critique of Pure Reason*. Cambridge University Press, 1998.]
